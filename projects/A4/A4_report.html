Proposition: Women in authoritative power create better working conditions for their country.

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Precarious Work Axis scale: -1. No tic marks encourage readers to create their own mental scale and think, "Wow these countries have such little job insecurity. Must be nice to live in!" Additionally, only female job vulnerability data is considered, meaning this scale is inaccurate in response to the total population.
  • Removed Countries in Bar Chart: -2. Not all countries ≥25% women in parliament are part of the bar graph, to prevent outliers, and some are ommitted to retain narrative.
  • Pink color for positively-related data points: 1. While discouraged by this class when discussing gender, pink is often positively associated with pro-women-related movements (e.x. breast cancer awareness) therefore this color association promotes an inviting enviroment to supporters.
  • Two-Tone Women in Parliament Representation: -1. While it may seem awkward, I consdiered 50% as the optimal number of where the female gender would be in authorative power given a sex ratio. Countries ≥25% women in parliament would be considered nations "heading in the right direction" to better gender representation in government, therefore I believe is more earnest. However, a gradient would be more sufficient to visually explain how gender representation fluctuates from country to country, as there is little graph variaton.
  • Title: -1. There is a clear narrative, though could be considered tone-deaf as diversity exists in many facets (race, disability, sexual orientation) other than gender. This could be trying to grab attention of those any underrepresented diverse group, despite only focusing one type of demographic.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Living in Polluted Air Scale: -2 This chart at first glance easily paints a narrative, as it seems the living (therefore working) conditions of a countyr of considerably terrible. However, after receognizing multiple country codes (Australia, Netherlands, etc.) and comparing its percent value to Finland, with prior knowledge I would be relatively skeptical about how this data is portrayed. Originally, I had planned to use a different graph depicting labor force perecentages and vulnerability
  • Top Chart: Mapping Urban Population scale: 2 No information is this chart is manipulated. The chart is easily readible, and corresponds with the second data point emphasized in the bottom graph.
  • Data Point Imagery: -1. The usage of exclamation points is relatively dramatic. It seems the top chart has very negative data given the title's sacrasm, despite it presenting a simple fact of urbanization in various ≥25% women-parliament countries.
  • Living in Polluted Air Chart Color: -1.Red is often associated with the feelings of "stop" or "negative". Alongside the fact the bars look like blood lines dripping, it could indicate how little the women in parliament "don't care" about resulting health conditions from air pollution. I would love to try an upside down axis, mentioned in the original article about "Gun Deaths in Florida" to play with persuasive visualizations.
  • Title: -2. The title could do without sarcasm. If this chart was paired with an article, it could be easily guided to an article against progressive female representation in government, or simply those that think highlighting it is ineffective. It cases to audience, rather than letting the visual remain objective.

Final Reflection

This design process of creating persuasive visuals was relatively difficult. Trends to go against propositions were fairly common, not to mention when trends did not exist at all. Countries contain different living conditions and cultural norms, so providing a worldwide conclusion on a social phemonena was not easy to create. Contrastly, I did not expect how Tableau works against data manipulation. There was an extent to what Tableau allowed you to manipulate data with fromat, axis and grouping. You could not just remove color:value correspondence without completely manipulating raw data. In addition, Tableau would often remove previously-available options if data representation would not make senese. It makes me wonder how much ethical practice is engrained into Tableau software development.

Besides my technical experience, I found "ethical analysis" to be a relatively easy line to cross over. Misinformation over comes from words, in which data (visually represented) is used to refute it-- yet data can also be missrepresented as well! This encourages me to be better questioning data visuals, instead assuming, "It's in a graph, so it must be correct." Ethical analysis, personally means the most optimal way to represent data from a table into colors and shapes. This should little to no manipulation when presenting, only for visual effects. If a table (CSV/Excel spreadsheet) has a trend, that visual must share that result as well. It's hard to draw boundaries on "acceptable" persuasive choices, because when two sides to an argument exist, an opposing side to an argument can understand any part of your visual to be "unacceptable" and "problematic". I think the most important bound in general is, if readers have to squint because they feel they don't have the full picture/are being misled, then you have failed at making a good visualization.
